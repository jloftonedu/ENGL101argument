The Glass Menagerie Quiz

Test your understanding of Tennessee Williams' modernist techniques and Scene I

1According to the notes, Tennessee Williams was writing during a shift from which literary movement to modernism?

Correct Answer: Realism. The notes specifically state that Williams was writing during "a shift from the realism movement to modernism."

2Which of the following is NOT mentioned as a tenant of modernism in the notes?

Correct Answer: Focus on universal moral truths. Modernist writers actually focused on "the psychology of the individual rather than concern with universal truths."

3What three theatrical conventions does Tennessee Williams use throughout the play according to his "Production Notes"?

Correct Answer: Screen device, Music, Lighting. These are the three conventions specifically mentioned in Williams' Production Notes.

4Why does Williams provide Tom as the narrator of the play?

Correct Answer: Because it's a "memory play" told from Tom's perspective. The notes emphasize that this is a memory play, meaning events are filtered through Tom's recollection rather than presented objectively.

5What does the setting description "an alley in St. Louis, an apartment that is small" suggest about the Wingfield family's circumstances?

Correct Answer: They live in cramped, modest conditions. The description of a small apartment in an alley suggests economic constraints and limited living space.

6What does Laura's final line in Scene I, "Mother's afraid I'm going to be an old maid," reveal about the family's concerns?

Correct Answer: Amanda fears Laura won't find a husband. The term "old maid" refers to an unmarried woman, indicating Amanda's concern about Laura's marital prospects.

7According to the notes, modernist writers were influenced by which psychologist's work?

Correct Answer: Sigmund Freud. The notes mention that writers were influenced by "the complexity of human relationships after Sigmund Freud publishes The Interpretation of Dreams."

8Short Answer: Based on the notes, explain what it means that The Glass Menagerie is a "memory play" and how this affects our understanding of the events presented.

Sample Answer: A "memory play" means that the events are not presented as objective reality but are filtered through Tom's recollections. This means the play is "not exactly as it happened but from the perspective of Tom," who voices the importance of the scenes. As memories, the events may be subjective, emotionally colored, or emphasize certain moments over others based on their significance to Tom. This technique aligns with modernist tendencies to focus on individual psychology rather than universal truths.

9Short Answer: What is a "gentleman caller" in the context of the play, and why might this concept be important to understanding Amanda's character and concerns?

Sample Answer: A "gentleman caller" refers to a potential male suitor who would visit a young woman with romantic intentions, often as a precursor to courtship and marriage. This concept is important to understanding Amanda because it represents her hopes for Laura's future and her fears about Laura remaining unmarried. Amanda's concern about Laura becoming an "old maid" shows how important she considers it for Laura to attract a gentleman caller, reflecting the social expectations and pressures on women during this time period.

10Short Answer: How did the historical context (post-WWI anxiety and Freud's influence) shape the way modernist writers like Tennessee Williams approached their craft?

Sample Answer: Post-WWI anxiety about foreign and domestic issues created uncertainty and disillusionment that led writers to question traditional narrative techniques and social conventions. Freud's work on the unconscious mind and dream interpretation encouraged writers to explore the complexity of human psychology and relationships. As a result, modernist writers like Williams "revolted against established writing techniques" and sought new perspectives that focused on "the psychology of the individual rather than concern with universal truths." This led to techniques like stream of consciousness, fragmented perception, and non-linear time that we see in Williams' work.
