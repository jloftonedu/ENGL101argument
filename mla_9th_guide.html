Finding Credible Sources

What Makes a Source Credible?

Credible sources are reliable, accurate, and authoritative. When evaluating sources, consider the CRAAP Test:

  • Currency: How recent is the information?
  • Relevance: Does it relate to your topic?
  • Authority: Who is the author and what are their credentials?
  • Accuracy: Is the information supported by evidence?
  • Purpose: Why was this source created?

Types of Credible Sources

Academic Sources

Peer-reviewed journal articles, scholarly books, conference proceedings

Primary Sources

Original documents, interviews, surveys, historical records

Government Publications

Reports, statistics, official documents from government agencies

Reputable News Sources

Established newspapers, magazines, and news websites with editorial standards

Where to Find Sources

  • Academic databases: JSTOR, ProQuest, EBSCOhost, PubMed
  • Library catalogs: Your institution's library system
  • Google Scholar: Free academic search engine
  • Government websites: .gov sites for official information
  • Professional organizations: Industry-specific resources

Research Tip

Start with your library's research guides for your subject area. Librarians curate the best resources for each discipline.

MLA Core Elements

MLA 9th edition uses nine core elements to create citations. Not every source will have all elements, but include those that are available in this order:

1. Author

Person, group, or organization responsible for the work

2. Title of Source

Full title of the work being cited

3. Title of Container

Larger work that contains your source

4. Contributors

Other people who helped create the work

5. Version

Edition, director's cut, expanded version, etc.

6. Number

Volume, issue, season, episode numbers

7. Publisher

Organization that produced or distributed the source

8. Publication Date

When the work was published or updated

9. Location

Page numbers, URLs, DOIs, physical location

Template Format

Author. "Title of Source." Title of Container, Contributors, Version, Number, Publisher, Publication Date, Location.

In-Text Citations

In-text citations appear within your paper and direct readers to the full citation on your Works Cited page. They typically include the author's last name and page number.

Basic Format

Author-Page Format

Climate change represents "the most significant threat to global stability" (Johnson 45).

OR

Johnson argues that climate change represents "the most significant threat to global stability" (45).

Variations

No Author

The study reveals concerning trends ("Global Warming" 12).

Multiple Authors

Recent research supports this conclusion (Smith and Jones 78).

For three or more authors: (Smith et al. 78)

No Page Numbers (Web Sources)

Online education has transformed learning (Davis).

Indirect Source

Einstein believed in the power of imagination (qtd. in Wilson 234).

Avoid Plagiarism

Every idea, quote, or paraphrase from another source must be cited, even if you put it in your own words.

Works Cited Page

The Works Cited page lists all sources referenced in your paper. It appears on a separate page at the end of your document.

Formatting Guidelines

  • Start on a new page
  • Center the title "Works Cited" at the top
  • Double-space throughout
  • Use hanging indent for each entry
  • Alphabetize by author's last name (or title if no author)
  • Use Times New Roman, 12-point font

Sample Entries

Book

Morrison, Toni. Beloved. Knopf, 1987.

Journal Article

Chen, Michael. "Digital Privacy in the Modern Era." Technology Review, vol. 45, no. 3, 2023, pp. 12-28.

Website

Williams, Sarah. "Climate Change Solutions." Environmental Action, 15 Mar. 2023, www.enviroaction.org/climate-solutions.

Newspaper Article

Rodriguez, Maria. "Economic Recovery Shows Promise." The Washington Post, 20 Feb. 2023, p. A1.

Integrating Sources Effectively

Good source integration weaves evidence seamlessly into your argument while maintaining your voice as the primary author.

The Integration Formula

  1. Introduce: Set up the source with context
  2. Present: Quote, paraphrase, or summarize
  3. Explain: Analyze and connect to your argument

Signal Phrases

Use signal phrases to introduce your sources and show their relationship to your argument:

Purpose Signal Phrases
Agreement agrees, confirms, supports, validates
Disagreement contradicts, disputes, challenges, refutes
Neutrality states, reports, observes, notes
Emphasis emphasizes, stresses, highlights, underscores

Effective Integration Example

While some critics dismiss renewable energy as unrealistic, leading climate scientist Dr. Sarah Chen emphasizes that "solar and wind power have become economically competitive with fossil fuels in most markets" (Chen 45). This economic shift demonstrates that environmental and financial interests now align, making the transition to clean energy not just morally imperative but economically logical.

Avoiding Drop Quotes

Poor Integration (Drop Quote)

Climate change is a serious problem. "Global temperatures have risen by 1.2°C since pre-industrial times" (IPCC 23). This is concerning.

Better Integration

The urgency of addressing climate change becomes clear when examining recent temperature data. According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, "Global temperatures have risen by 1.2°C since pre-industrial times" (IPCC 23), a seemingly small increase that scientists warn represents a dangerous acceleration of warming trends.

Quoting Guidelines

Use quotes sparingly and strategically. Quote when the author's exact words are particularly powerful, precise, or when you need to analyze specific language.

When to Quote

  • The language is particularly striking or memorable
  • You're analyzing the author's word choice or style
  • The author is a recognized authority
  • You need to preserve the exact meaning

Short Quotes (fewer than 4 lines)

Integrate short quotes directly into your text using quotation marks.

Short Quote Example

Maya Angelou reflects on resilience: "There is no greater agony than bearing an untold story inside you" (Angelou 127).

Long Quotes (4+ lines)

Format long quotes as block quotes: start on a new line, indent 1 inch from the left margin, double-space, and omit quotation marks.

Block Quote Example

Martin Luther King Jr. eloquently describes the urgency of civil rights:

We have waited for more than 340 years for our constitutional and God-given rights. The nations of Asia and Africa are moving with jetlike speed toward gaining political independence, but we still creep at horse-and-buggy pace toward gaining a cup of coffee at a lunch counter. (King 289)

This metaphor powerfully illustrates the frustration felt by those fighting for equality.

Modifying Quotes

Adding Words [brackets]

The researcher concluded that "[the] results clearly support the hypothesis" (Johnson 45).

Omitting Words ...ellipses

Lincoln proclaimed that "government of the people ... shall not perish from the earth" (Lincoln 123).

Quote Integration Tip

Never end a paragraph with a quote. Always follow quotes with your own analysis or explanation of their significance.

Paraphrasing and Summarizing

Paraphrasing and summarizing allow you to present others' ideas in your own words while maintaining your writing voice.

Paraphrasing

Paraphrasing restates a specific passage in your own words, typically about the same length as the original.

Original Text

"The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has created both unprecedented opportunities and significant ethical challenges for society."

Effective Paraphrase

Society faces both remarkable possibilities and serious moral dilemmas as AI technology develops at an accelerated pace (Chen 78).

Summarizing

Summarizing condenses larger amounts of information into key points, focusing on main ideas rather than details.

Summary Example

In her comprehensive study of remote work trends, Martinez (2023) found that companies implementing flexible work policies saw increased employee satisfaction, reduced overhead costs, and improved talent retention, though some struggled with maintaining team cohesion and company culture.

Paraphrasing Tips

  • Read the original multiple times to understand the meaning
  • Put the original away and write in your own words
  • Change both vocabulary and sentence structure
  • Check that you've maintained the original meaning
  • Always cite the source, even when paraphrasing

Avoid Patchwork Paraphrasing

Don't just substitute synonyms. Completely restructure the sentence and express the idea in your natural writing voice.

Common Source Types

Here are citation formats for the most commonly used sources in academic writing:

Books

Single Author

Gladwell, Malcolm. Outliers: The Story of Success. Little, Brown, 2008.

Multiple Authors

Gilbert, Sandra M., and Susan Gubar. The Madwoman in the Attic. Yale University Press, 1979.

Edited Book

Ramazani, Jahan, editor. The Norton Anthology of Modern and Contemporary Poetry. 3rd ed., Norton, 2003.

Journal Articles

Print Journal

Hartley, John. "The Evolution of Digital Media." Media Studies Quarterly, vol. 34, no. 2, 2023, pp. 45-62.

Online Journal

Thompson, Lisa. "Social Media and Mental Health." Psychology Today, vol. 28, no. 4, 2023, pp. 12-25, doi:10.1037/pst0000123.

Websites

Web Page

National Institute of Health. "Understanding Depression." NIH, 10 Jan. 2023, www.nih.gov/depression-guide.

Online Newspaper

Baker, James. "Economic Trends in 2023." The New York Times, 15 Feb. 2023, www.nytimes.com/2023/02/15/business/economic-trends.html.

Other Sources

Film/Documentary

An Inconvenient Truth. Directed by Davis Guggenheim, Paramount Pictures, 2006.

Interview

Smith, Jane. Personal interview. 5 Mar. 2023.

Government Document

United States, Department of Education. "Student Achievement Report." GPO, 2023.

Citation Help

When in doubt about citation format, consult the MLA Handbook (9th edition) or reputable online resources like the Purdue OWL (Online Writing Lab).
